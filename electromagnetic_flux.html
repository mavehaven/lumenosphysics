Faraday's Law of Induction:

\[ \mathcal{E} = -N \frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} \]

Where:

  • \(\mathcal{E}\) = Induced electromotive force (EMF) in volts
  • \(N\) = Number of turns in the coil
  • \(\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}\) = Rate of change of magnetic flux

Magnetic Flux: \(\Phi_B = B \cdot A \cdot \cos\theta\)

  • \(B\) = Magnetic field strength in teslas
  • \(A\) = Area of the coil in m¬≤ (\(A = \pi r^2\))
  • \(\theta\) = Angle between magnetic field and normal to surface

The negative sign represents Lenz's Law: the induced EMF creates a current whose magnetic field opposes the change in flux that produced it.

Advanced Concepts:

  • The magnetic field strength decreases with the square of distance (\(B \propto \frac{1}{r^2}\))
  • Maximum flux change occurs when the magnet moves perpendicular to the coil's axis
  • Induced current is proportional to the rate of flux change
  • Lenz's Law ensures conservation of energy - the induced current opposes the motion creating it

Magnetic Field Model:

\[ B(r) = \frac{B_0 \cdot a^2}{(r^2 + a^2)^{3/2}} \]

Where:

  • \(B_0\) = Magnetic field strength at magnet surface
  • \(a\) = Characteristic length of the magnet (radius)
  • \(r\) = Distance from magnet center

Real-world Applications:

  • Electric Generators: Convert mechanical energy to electrical energy through electromagnetic induction
  • Transformers: Transfer electrical energy between circuits through changing magnetic flux
  • Induction Cooktops: Use induced currents to generate heat in cookware
  • Electric Guitar Pickups: Convert string vibrations to electrical signals

Experiments to Try:

  1. Increase magnet speed and observe how EMF peaks become higher and narrower
  2. Add more coil turns to see amplification of the induced EMF
  3. Adjust magnetic field strength to simulate different magnet strengths
  4. Pause when the magnet is near the coil to examine flux relationships
