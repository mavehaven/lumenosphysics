Classical Mechanics

Introduction

Classical mechanics, developed primarily in the 17th to 19th centuries, describes the motion of macroscopic objects under the influence of forces. It provides the foundation for understanding everything from the motion of planets to the design of modern machinery.

Historical Context

Ancient Greece (400-300 BCE)

Aristotle's early theories on motion and gravity, though later disproven, laid the groundwork for systematic study of physics.

Renaissance (1500s)

Galileo's experiments challenged Aristotelian physics and established the scientific method.

Scientific Revolution (1600s)

Newton unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics with his laws of motion and universal gravitation.

Key Concepts & Equations

Newton's Laws of Motion

First Law (Inertia)

An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by a net force.

ΣF = 0
Second Law (Force & Acceleration)

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it.

F = ma
Third Law (Action-Reaction)

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

F₁₂ = -F₂₁

Energy & Work

Work

The work done by a force is the product of the force and the displacement in its direction.

W = F·d
Kinetic Energy

The energy possessed by an object due to its motion.

KE = ½mv²
Potential Energy

The energy possessed by an object due to its position or configuration.

PE = mgh

Historical Development

Pythagoras (6th century BCE)

Discovered the mathematical relationship between string length and musical pitch, laying the foundation for wave theory.

Christiaan Huygens (1678)

Proposed the wave theory of light, challenging Newton's particle theory.

Thomas Young (1801)

Demonstrated wave interference through the famous double-slit experiment.
